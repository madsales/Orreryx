Is Taiwan Part of China?

The answer depends entirely on who you ask. Here is the actual legal and political situation:

Beijing's Position

Taiwan is a province of China

The People's Republic of China claims Taiwan under its "One China" principle and considers reunification — by force if necessary — a core national interest enshrined in the PRC constitution.

Taiwan's Position

Self-governing democracy since 1949

Taiwan (Republic of China) has its own elected president, parliament, military, constitution and currency. Most Taiwanese identify as Taiwanese, not Chinese, and support maintaining the status quo.

US Position

Intentional ambiguity

The US "acknowledges" (not endorses) the One China position, maintains unofficial relations via the American Institute in Taiwan, and sells Taiwan defensive weapons under the Taiwan Relations Act.

International Law

Not a UN member state

Taiwan lost its UN seat to the PRC in 1971. It is not a member of most international organizations but functions as a fully independent state in practice with formal relations with 13 countries.

Why Does China Want Taiwan?

Strategic

First Island Chain & Pacific Access

Taiwan sits at the heart of the first island chain, blocking China's naval access to the open Pacific. Controlling Taiwan would give the PLA Navy strategic depth for power projection.

Economic

TSMC & Semiconductor Control

Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Company (TSMC) produces over 90% of the world's most advanced chips. Controlling TSMC would give China leverage over global technology supply chains.

Political

National Rejuvenation Narrative

Reunification with Taiwan is central to Xi Jinping's "Chinese Dream" of national rejuvenation. Failure to reunify is seen as an existential threat to CCP political legitimacy.

Historical

Civil War Unfinished Business

The CCP and ROC (Taiwan) fought in China's civil war (1927-1949). The ROC government fled to Taiwan in 1949. Beijing considers reunification the completion of the civil war.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is Taiwan part of China?
Legally disputed. China claims Taiwan as a province under its One China policy. Taiwan has self-governed as a separate democracy since 1949 with its own military, currency, president and constitution. The US and most Western countries maintain unofficial relations with Taiwan without recognising it as a sovereign state. The United Nations recognises the PRC, not Taiwan, as China's representative.
Will China invade Taiwan?
Most analysts assess a full-scale invasion as possible but not imminent. Key deterrents include: US military commitment under the Taiwan Relations Act, the enormous economic cost of sanctions (far larger than those on Russia), and the high military risk of an amphibious assault. PLA military exercises around Taiwan have intensified significantly since 2022. Orreryx tracks all China-Taiwan military incidents on its live map in real time.
Why does China want Taiwan?
Four main reasons: (1) National reunification is central to CCP political identity and Xi Jinping's "Chinese Dream". (2) Taiwan's location controls access to the Pacific through the first island chain. (3) TSMC in Taiwan produces 90%+ of the world's most advanced semiconductors — critical geopolitical leverage. (4) The CCP views unresolved Taiwan status as unfinished business from the Chinese Civil War.
What is the Taiwan Strait?
The Taiwan Strait is a 180km-wide body of water between Taiwan and mainland China. Approximately 50% of global container shipping passes through it. The PLA regularly crosses the strait's median line during military exercises. The US Navy conducts freedom of navigation operations through the strait, which China protests as provocations.
How would a China-Taiwan war affect global markets?
It would be catastrophic: TSMC disruption would halt global chip supply for years, affecting every industry from cars to smartphones. Taiwan Strait closure would disrupt 50% of global shipping. US-China sanctions would be the largest in history. Defence stocks, gold and USD would surge. Tech stocks, Asian equities and global trade would collapse.
